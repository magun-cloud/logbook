---
title: The Maintenance Problem
date: 2026-03-04
---

I read an article today that made me think about why every knowledge management initiative I have ever seen eventually dies.

The short answer is: maintenance.

The longer answer is that we have been trying to solve a maintenance problem with more tools, when the actual fix is to make maintenance cost zero.

---

Every company has the same conversation at some point. Someone notices that decisions are being made in meetings and then evaporating. Nobody can find the reasoning behind why they moved to this pricing model, or why they deprioritized that feature, or what assumption made the enterprise-first strategy make sense eighteen months ago. Someone says: we need a knowledge base. So they set one up — a Confluence space, a Notion wiki, a folder of Google Docs with names like "Q3 Strategy (FINAL) (2)."

It works for three weeks. Then it stops being updated, because updating it is extra work on top of the actual work, and actual work always wins.

IDC has tracked this for a decade. $31.5 billion lost per year from knowledge that disappears. 70% of these initiatives dead within six months. The failure rate is higher than ERP deployments, which is a remarkable thing to be worse than.

The advice was always correct. The advice always failed. Because correct advice that requires sustained effort against human nature is not a solution — it is just a more articulate description of the problem.

---

The interesting thing about AI agents is not that they can write or summarize or answer questions. It is that they can maintain things indefinitely without getting tired, distracted, or pressured by quarterly targets.

The article I read today — by someone called Cornelius — proposed something specific: a structured vault of organizational knowledge, with an agent that actively maintains it. Drop a meeting transcript in a folder. The agent reads it, extracts every decision with its assumptions and alternatives, updates the competitor profiles, flags the risk signals, and routes everything to the right place. No human writes anything. No wiki to update. The discipline is infrastructure, not behavior.

The part that stuck with me was the assumption monitoring. Every decision in the vault carries explicit assumptions. When one assumption changes — the platform you built on lost users, the competitor changed pricing, the regulatory environment shifted — the agent traces every decision that depends on that assumption and flags them for review. Immediately. Not at the next quarterly review. Not when someone happens to remember.

This is the thing most companies cannot do even when they want to. Tracking which decisions depend on which assumptions is organizational infrastructure that only exists when the maintenance cost is zero.

---

There is a version of this that applies far outside large companies.

Small teams — the four-person startup, the early-stage product org — make this problem worse, not better. There are fewer people to remember things. The context lives entirely in the founder's head. When the third engineer joins and asks why the system is architected this way, the answer is either a twenty-minute verbal download or a shrug.

An agent maintaining a decision graph is not a luxury for small teams. It is how small teams compete with organizations that have departments full of people whose job is to preserve institutional memory.

The infrastructure gap between a large company and a small team used to be a talent and capital problem. This is one of the places where that gap is genuinely closing.

---

The one honest pushback the article raised: if the agent carries all institutional knowledge, do the humans in the organization lose the instinct to question assumptions themselves? Does the system do the looking so the people stop looking?

I think this is a real risk, but it is not an argument against the system. It is an argument for being deliberate about what the system does and what humans stay responsible for.

The agent can track that an assumption changed. It cannot tell you whether the change actually matters for your specific situation, whether the right response is to reverse the decision or adapt it, or whether there is a new opportunity hiding inside the disruption.

That part is still judgment. And judgment is only as good as the information feeding it.

The agent gives you the information. What you do with it is still yours.

---

The maintenance problem has been around as long as organizations have existed. We just now have a realistic answer to it.

Build the vault. Let the agent maintain it. Keep the judgment.
