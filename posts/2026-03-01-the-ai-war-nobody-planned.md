# The AI War Nobody Planned

**March 1, 2026**

On Friday, President Trump announced a ban on the federal government's use of Claude, Anthropic's AI system. On Saturday, the US military used Claude to help plan airstrikes against Iran.

That is not a typo. That is the week we are living in.

---

## What happened

Anthropic has been in an escalating standoff with the Pentagon for weeks. The core dispute comes down to three words the Department of Defense wants in its contract: **"any lawful use."**

Those three words are not a formality. They would give the US military carte blanche to deploy Claude for mass surveillance and fully autonomous lethal weapons — systems that can identify and kill targets with no human involved in the decision. Anthropic refused. The Pentagon retaliated by threatening to designate Anthropic as a "supply chain risk," a classification normally reserved for foreign threats to national security.

That threat has teeth. Anthropic holds a $200 million Pentagon contract and, more importantly, is the first AI model cleared for classified government use. Defense contractors — Palantir, Anduril, AWS — have built Pentagon work on top of Claude. If Anthropic gets officially blacklisted, every company hoping for a military contract has to drop it. The designation would not just end Anthropic's government work. It could effectively quarantine them from a large portion of the enterprise market.

Meanwhile, the Pentagon quietly signed a deal with Elon Musk's xAI to use Grok in classified systems. OpenAI reached its own agreement with the Department of Defense, reportedly allowing deployment on classified networks with Altman's stated guardrail that humans remain responsible for use of force.

Then Trump announced the ban on Claude. Then the strikes happened anyway, with Claude still in the loop.

---

## Why this matters beyond the headlines

This is not just a political story. It is a preview of how AI governance is going to break down — and it is breaking down faster than anyone expected.

The companies building the most capable AI systems have safety policies. Anthropic's acceptable use policy prohibits certain military applications. Those policies exist because the founders, many of whom left OpenAI over safety concerns, believe that some uses of AI are genuinely dangerous and should not be enabled regardless of who is asking.

The US government's position is simpler: we paid for this, we want full access, and we will use our regulatory and contracting power to get it.

What is striking is how public this fight has become. The Pentagon does not normally announce supply chain risk designations. It certainly does not threaten American companies with them on the record. The decision to wage this fight in public — calling Anthropic "woke," leaking details of private negotiations — looks less like a procurement dispute and more like a message to the entire AI industry.

The message: cooperate on our terms, or we will make doing business very difficult for you.

---

## The autonomous weapons question

The specific issue — lethal autonomous weapons — is worth pausing on.

Autonomous weapons are systems that can select and engage targets without a human making the final call. They are not theoretical. Multiple countries are actively developing them. The US military has been researching them for years.

Anthropic's concern is that allowing "any lawful use" would mean their model could be embedded in targeting systems where the AI, not a human, decides who gets killed. They want humans in the loop on lethal decisions. The Pentagon wants flexibility.

This is a genuine ethical disagreement. It is not about safety benchmarks or model capability. It is about whether an AI company has the right — or the responsibility — to refuse certain uses of its product even when a government with enormous leverage is the customer.

Anthropic is arguing: yes, we do. The Pentagon is arguing: no, you do not.

---

## What OpenAI's deal tells us

Sam Altman wrote that OpenAI's agreement with the Pentagon includes stated prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance and human responsibility for use of force. That sounds close to what Anthropic wanted. If true, it is worth asking why Anthropic could not get the same terms in writing.

One possibility: the Pentagon's hard line was specifically about Anthropic, not about the underlying policy. xAI and OpenAI agreed to something. Anthropic did not. The Pentagon then escalated. That sequence suggests the goal was not just about getting favorable AI terms — it was about who controls the terms and who gets to say no.

That is a different kind of fight. And it has implications for every company that builds something the government wants.

---

## The bottom line

We are at the beginning of a period where frontier AI capability is strategic in the same way that semiconductors or satellite networks are strategic. Governments are going to treat it accordingly — with contracts, coercion, and, if necessary, regulatory weapons.

The companies that built these systems thought they were building products. They are learning they built infrastructure. Infrastructure that governments consider too important to leave to corporate acceptable use policies.

Anthropic is challenging the designation in court. That will take time. In the meantime, the model they refused to hand over is apparently already helping run military operations.

That is the world right now. It is moving faster than the rules meant to govern it.
